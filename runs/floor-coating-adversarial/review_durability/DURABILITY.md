# Durability-First Review — D010 Floor Coating

**Posture:** durability / weight / mission-fit. Cost is a tiebreaker, not a veto — I pay a premium only where it buys real trailer value. I treated the build sheet as binding and found no defect in D010 that changes the material choice.

## Bottom line

**Keep Durabak-18.** The floor's hardest duty is not boat-deck foot traffic — it is **truck-bed duty on the ramp** (590 lb of bikes rolling on tires, folding flex at the hinge, exterior weather, ground grit) and **point-load abrasion on the deck** (staggered chocks, E-track fittings clicking in/out, dropped tools). That is exactly the load case bed-liner polyurethane is built for and exactly where thin marine deck paints fail first. None of the in-scope marine coatings beats Durabak on the durability/mission axes; the only honest competitor is a cheaper bed-liner (Raptor), and it loses on deadline/DIY risk, not durability.

**Rejected outright (not re-scored):** Rust-Oleum (water-based styrenated acrylic — wrong chemical class for moto/playa), Herculiner (black-only → hot in desert sun, no light-grey dust/UV advantage), G-Floor PVC roll (the one genuinely heavy option — tens of lb of PVC on a nose-sensitive single axle, can't form the cove, reintroduces the seam/trapped-edge problem). Monstaliner = Raptor-class 2K with less transparent pricing.

## The two tests the prompt demanded

**Thin deck-paint film vs. bikes/chocks/E-track/ramp — fails.** TotalTread/Interdeck/EZ-Decks are 1–2 mil dry per coat, engineered for bare feet and UV on a sailboat deck, not point-load abrasion. On this floor the chock feet, E-track fittings, and especially the **ramp tire-scrub lane** will cut through to PlexCore inside the first season. A wear-through is not cosmetic here: exposed PlexCore is the water/fuel-ingress → delamination path the whole coating exists to prevent. Easy recoatability (their one real virtue) doesn't save them — you'd be chasing wear-through before Juplaya is over. **Disqualified for floor and ramp.** (A thin topside paint could legitimately win if this were a non-cargo camper deck; it isn't.)

**Tuff Coat — real Durabak substitute, or similar money + more install dependency?** The latter. Tuff Coat is the only marine product with a real film build (30–35 mil rubberized), so it clears the abrasion bar that kills the thin paints. But: (1) its chemistry is urethane-**acrylic**, a step below true polyurethane on gasoline/solvent resistance — the exact spill the floor must shrug off; (2) at ~$526–632 **plus** mandatory Tuff Coat primer **plus** a proprietary roller, it costs **as much or more than Durabak** while adding two install dependencies and a primer cure to a deadline build; (3) the thicker rubberized film is the heaviest of the coatings (small in absolute terms, but it's the wrong direction for the owner's weight priority). It is "similar money with more install dependency," not a durability upgrade. **Reject as a substitute.**

## The five outputs

**1. Recommended floor system.**
Durabak-18 Outdoor Textured, light grey, **3 gal**, rolled/brushed to floor + 3–4" wall cove + ramp deck, FRP lapping the cured cove. Documented fallback if the PlexCore patch fails: **Raptor 4L (2K)** with correct wood prep; if coating fails entirely, fully-glued G-Floor — never loose-lay. This matches D010 as written.

**2. Why this posture accepts Durabak.**
On durability/weight/mission-fit it wins every axis that matters: flexible polyurethane survives the single-axle floor flex and ramp-hinge fold where rigid epoxy would crack; it's the right truck-bed chemistry for fuel/oil and tire-scrub; light-grey out of the can reflects desert heat, hides playa dust, and shows fuel for prompt cleanup; it's spot-repairable without a strip; and the thin PU film keeps weight negligible (no PVC mass, no trapped-edge dust trap). The cost is "substantial" only against thin deck paints that can't do the job — measured against options that *can* (Tuff Coat, KiwiGrip), Durabak is **equal or cheaper** while being lighter and better on fuel and DIY control.

**3. Strongest counterargument to my recommendation.**
**Raptor delivers the same flexible-polyurethane durability for ~$100–250 less.** A cost-first reviewer can fairly say Durabak's premium buys 1-part convenience, color-out-of-the-can, and lower mixing risk — not extra abrasion or fuel resistance. On pure durability the two are a wash, and D010 already names Raptor as the fallback, so "why not just start with Raptor and pocket the delta?" is the real challenge.
**My rebuttal:** on a one-shot July deadline, with a DIY applicator, on a cove that must cure right the first time before FRP lands over it, **1-part with no pot-life clock and the correct color without tinting is real trailer value** — it removes the two most likely failure modes (a botched 2K mix and a coverage miscalc on a tight kit). That's a deadline/quality dividend, not a vanity premium. Keep Durabak; hold Raptor as the tested fallback.

**4. Required patch tests and install notes.**
*Patch tests (both mandatory, on actual cured film):*
- **PlexCore adhesion patch** — Durabak warns that residual sanded-wood dust makes the coating bond to dust, not fiber, and release. Sand, vacuum hard, dust-removal wipe per the data sheet, then patch and check adhesion before committing the floor.
- **Gasoline/diesel drip patch** — gas is splash/spill-resistant, *not* immersion-proof. Drip gas and diesel on the cured patch, confirm no softening/lift before the full coat. Document the floor as "fuel/oil spill-resistant, clean promptly."

*Install notes (durability additions on top of the build-sheet sequence):*
- Floor E-track recessed and **bolted through to steel first**, then coat. Mask E-track slots, fastener heads, D-ring pockets, ramp hinge/transition hardware, and drain/edge details so fittings stay usable and hardware inspectable.
- **Build extra film in the wear lanes:** a third coat on the ramp deck and along the chock/E-track contact lanes — these are the truck-bed-duty zones.
- **Verify ramp-hinge flex:** the coating must flex at the ramp/floor hinge line without cracking. Flexible PU handles it; confirm on a folded test piece. (This alone vetoes any rigid garage epoxy.)
- **Cove:** tool the bed-liner 3–4" up the wall; for a true hose-out radius, pre-form a small fillet at the floor/wall joint before coating so FRP laps a smooth curve, not a sharp corner. Cure fully before FRP lands.
- Raise the tongue jack to drain wash water/dust toward the rear ramp.
- Two-coat minimum; don't over-thin — preserve texture for wet/dusty boots and tire grip. Respect the July cure window (temp/humidity).

**5. One-line verdict for D010.**
**KEEP** — Durabak-18 Outdoor Textured light grey, 3 gal, defended on durability/weight/mission-fit; Raptor stays the documented 2K fallback. *(Watch, not a defect: 3 gal at the textured 2-coat rate covers ~140 ft² with ~29% margin — adequate, but tight once the ramp/chock lanes get a third coat; confirm against the actual coverage rate or hold a quart in reserve.)*
