# Floor-Coating Verdict — D010

**Reconciles:** durability review (KEEP Durabak), budget review (REPLACE → Herculiner black), install review (REPLACE → Raptor 2K). Vote split 1 keep / 2 replace, but the two replacements point at *different* products and each is defeated by a constraint the build has already ratified. The material decision stands; one quantity correction is required.

## 1. Final recommendation

**Durabak-18 Outdoor Textured, light grey — KEEP.** Roll/brush onto the ¾" PlexCore floor, 3–4" up the lower walls as a cove, and across the rear ramp deck; FRP laps the cured cove into a hose-out tray.

**Quantity: buy 4 gal (3 gal base coat + 1 gal reserve).** This is the one change to D010. The reserve gallon is earmarked for a third coat in the wear lanes (ramp deck + chock/E-track contact lanes); confirm the final count against the patch-test coverage rate before the order freeze.

**Documented fallbacks (unchanged):** Raptor 4L (2K) with correct wood prep if Durabak fails the adhesion patch; fully-glued G-Floor PVC if coating fails entirely — never loose-lay.

## 2. Scope and constraints — what the floor must survive

- **Area:** ~140 ft² = floor (81"×157", ~88 ft²) + 3–4" cove (~12–14 ft²) + ramp (78"×72", ~39 ft²), before masking/roller/touch-up loss.
- **Truck-bed duty, not boat-deck duty:** 590 lb of bikes rolling on tires up/down the ramp, staggered chocks, E-track fittings clicking in/out, dropped tools — point-load abrasion and ramp tire-scrub are the governing loads. A wear-through to bare PlexCore is the water/fuel-ingress → delamination path the coating exists to prevent; it is not cosmetic.
- **Flex:** single-axle floor movement and the ramp/floor hinge fold — the film must flex without cracking. **This vetoes any rigid garage epoxy.**
- **Chemical:** moto gasoline/diesel/oil drips — spill-resistant and cleanable, *not* immersion-proof.
- **Playa:** alkali dust that must hose out, not pack into texture; no loose sheet trapping dust/water against the wood.
- **Heat/visibility:** light grey is a documented mission requirement, not finish vanity — it reflects desert heat (the same logic as the Henry 887 *white* silicone roof and a break-even July AC energy budget), hides playa dust, and shows fuel for prompt cleanup.
- **Deadline + applicator:** one-shot DIY application before ~July 4; the cove must cure correctly the first time because **FRP and the entire interior sequence land on top of it.**

## 4. Cost ruling — is Durabak's premium justified?

**Yes, but narrowly, and on the right grounds.** The premium is **not** justified as buying extra abrasion or fuel resistance — on those axes Raptor and Monstaliner (true 2K PU) are a wash with Durabak, and that is exactly why Raptor is the documented fallback. What the ~$100–250 premium over Raptor actually buys is **the removal of the two highest-probability DIY failure modes on a deadline-critical, sequence-blocking cove:** a botched 2-part mix and a coverage miscalc on a tight kit. One-part, open-and-roll, no pot-life clock, correct color without tinting — on a one-shot July job by a first-time applicator, with FRP and the whole interior waiting on the cove to cure right, that is a deadline/quality dividend, not a vanity charge.

The premium over Herculiner (~$210–270) buys **light grey**, which this build has already ruled a mission requirement (heat rejection, dust hiding, fuel visibility) — not a comfort upgrade.

The budget review's core point survives as a real number — Durabak costs roughly double the cheapest acceptable PU — and it is the reason Raptor (cheaper, same durability class) is held as the *tested fallback* rather than discarded. If the owner would rather pocket the delta and accept the 2K workflow risk, Raptor grey is the sanctioned downgrade. The default stays Durabak.

## 5. Alternatives rejected — decisive reason each

- **Herculiner (budget's pick):** functionally adequate truck-bed PU at ~half price, but **black**. In a heat-sensitive, dust-heavy build whose climate strategy (white silicone roof, break-even AC budget) and floor design both turn on *light grey*, black is a mission strike, not a finish penalty. The budget review's own escape hatch ("if black is a deal-breaker, upgrade to Raptor grey, **not** Durabak") concedes Herculiner fails the moment color is treated as a requirement — which the build already does.
- **Raptor 2K (install's pick):** kept as the fallback, rejected as the *starting* choice. Its genuine advantage — faster, more predictable 2K cure for the deadline — is folded into the Durabak sequence as a cure-window caution; but its 2-part mix + pot-life + tight one-kit coverage + spray-leaning workflow is a worse one-shot DIY risk on a cove that gates the whole interior than Durabak's manageable (shop-controlled) humidity sensitivity.
- **Tuff Coat:** the only marine product with a real film build (clears the thin-paint abrasion bar), but urethane-**acrylic** (weaker on the exact gasoline spill the floor must shrug off), and at ~$526–632 **plus** mandatory primer **plus** proprietary roller it costs as much or more than Durabak while adding install dependencies and a humidity-slave cure. Similar money, more risk — not an upgrade.
- **KiwiGrip:** water-based acrylic — softens under moto drips (vendor: fuel/oil/gas "should not pool"), abrades under point loads, and its aggressive texture traps playa dust. Wrong chemistry and wrong texture, at non-budget price.
- **TotalBoat TotalTread / Interlux Interdeck / Pettit EZ-Decks:** thin (1–2 mil) marine deck paints engineered for bare feet and UV, not cargo. They wear through to PlexCore at chocks, E-track, and the ramp tire-scrub lane inside the first season; paint can't build a cove fillet. Easy recoatability can't outrun the wear rate. (A legitimate winner for a non-cargo camper deck; this is not that.)
- **Rust-Oleum truck-bed coating:** cheapest, but water-based styrenated acrylic — wrong chemical class for moto/playa, too thin, metal-oriented. Price can't rescue the chemistry.
- **Monstaliner:** a credible roller-optimized 2K urethane, Raptor-class on every axis — but with less transparent current pricing and no advantage over the fallback the build already names. No reason to substitute it for Raptor.
- **G-Floor PVC:** the one genuinely heavy option (tens of lb of PVC on a nose-sensitive single axle), can't form the cove, and reintroduces the trapped-edge dust/water problem the coating exists to eliminate. Demoted to last-resort fallback, **glued only — never loose-lay.**

## 6. Required doc change to D010 / build sheet

**Material decision stands — no change to product or chemistry.** D010 remains Durabak-18 Outdoor Textured, light grey.

**Change the quantity line.** Current D010 / build sheet reads "buy **3 gallons**." Update to:

> **Buy 4 gal: 3 gal base coat + 1 gal reserve.** The reserve is committed to a **third coat in the wear lanes** — the ramp deck and the chock/E-track contact lanes (truck-bed-duty zones). Confirm the final count against the **patch-test coverage rate** before the order freeze; do not stretch or thin coverage to make 3 gal reach.

Rationale: all three reviews independently flagged that 3 gal is *tight*. At ~60 ft²/gal for two coats, 3 gal ≈ 180 ft² nominal against a ~140 ft² scope — adequate for two coats, but the durability-driven third coat in the ramp + chock lanes (~55–60 ft²) consumes the remaining margin. Carrying the fourth gallon removes the only "watch" item on an otherwise sound decision; any unused reserve becomes touch-up stock.

**Reinforce (already implied in the sequence, make explicit):** respect Durabak's moisture-cure window — land FRP only after the cove is tack-free and fully cured (≥24 h after tack-free; full hardness 3–5 days), so the interior sequence isn't built on a soft cove.

Per the run scope, **this verdict does not edit the build sheet or DECISION_LOG** — it states the change for the owner to ratify into D010 and design-freeze item 8.

## 7. Install gates that must remain (hard gates — non-negotiable)

1. **PlexCore adhesion patch** — sand, vacuum hard, dust-removal wipe per the data sheet, then patch and verify bond before committing the floor. Durabak warns residual sanded-wood dust makes the film bond to dust and release. Sheet peel on the patch = reject Durabak, go to Raptor with correct wood prep.
2. **Gasoline/diesel drip patch** — on cured film, drip gas and diesel as realistic spills (not immersion), wipe promptly, confirm no softening/lift/swell. Document the floor as **fuel/oil spill-resistant, clean promptly** — never "fuel-proof."

**Supporting install discipline (carry forward):**
- **Sequence:** finish the floor E-track layout → recess and bolt E-track **through to steel first** → sand/prep/patch → coat floor + cove + ramp → cure fully → FRP laps the cured cove.
- **Mask:** E-track slots (inside the track too), fastener heads, D-ring pockets, ramp hinge/transition hardware, drain/edge details — so fittings stay clickable and hardware inspectable.
- **Ramp-hinge flex check:** confirm the cured film flexes at the ramp/floor hinge without cracking (this is what vetoes rigid epoxy).
- **Cove:** pre-form a small fillet at the floor/wall joint; use a high-density foam roller and don't over-thin, to prevent sag/pooling at the bend; FRP lands on a smooth cured radius.
- **Build extra film in the wear lanes** (the reserve-gallon third coat); raise the tongue jack so wash water/dust drains toward the rear ramp.

## One-line verdict

**KEEP** Durabak-18 Outdoor Textured light grey for D010 — defended on durability, weight, mission-fit, and one-shot-deadline DIY control; **quantity corrected to 4 gal (3 + 1 reserve)** for the wear-lane third coat; Raptor 4L (2K) remains the tested fallback; the PlexCore adhesion and fuel/diesel-drip patches remain hard gates.
